# On Writing Poetry and Studying Medicine

*September 2024*

---

People often look surprised when I tell them I write poems.

*"But you want to be a doctor,"* they say, as if the two live in different universes.

I have come to believe they share the same address.

## The Discipline of Attention

Both poetry and medicine begin with the same act: **paying close attention**.

A poet looks at a rice field at dusk and notices the way the light bends at the edges of each stalk. A physician looks at a patient's face and notices the slight asymmetry that might indicate a neurological event.

Both are trained observers. Both are looking for meaning in details that most people walk past.

When I study the layers of the skin — epidermis, dermis, hypodermis — I cannot help but think of how a poem also has layers. The surface meaning, the emotional meaning, the meaning that only arrives the third time you read it.

## Language as Medicine

Words heal. This is not metaphor, or at least not only metaphor.

There is strong evidence now that the way a doctor communicates affects patient outcomes. A physician who explains clearly, listens deeply, and acknowledges pain — that physician is not just being polite. They are practicing a form of care that has measurable biological effects.

Placebo is one example. Connection is another.

The writers I admire most — Anton Chekhov, who was a doctor; William Carlos Williams, who delivered babies while writing poems — understood this. Their art and their medicine fed each other.

## What Writing Has Taught Me for Medicine

Writing poetry has made me:

1. **More precise with language** — every word in a poem must earn its place, just as every diagnosis must be supported by evidence
2. **More comfortable with ambiguity** — poems often do not resolve neatly, and neither do many clinical cases
3. **More empathetic** — inhabiting characters and voices on the page makes it easier to inhabit a patient's perspective

## What Medicine Has Taught Me for Writing

Studying biology and medicine has made me:

1. **Grounded in the body** — my poems are full of blood and bone and breath, not in a morbid way but in a living way
2. **Aware of stakes** — when you know what it costs a body to survive, every story becomes more urgent
3. **Respectful of process** — good medicine, like good writing, is never rushed

---

*The pen and the stethoscope are not opposites. They are, I think, cousins — both trying to make invisible things visible.*
