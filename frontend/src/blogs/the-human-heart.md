# The Human Heart: More Than a Pump

*October 2024*

---

In your chest, right now, a fist-sized muscle is contracting approximately **70 times per minute**. It has been doing this since before you were born. It will do it until the last moment of your life.

We call it the heart. Science calls it a double-pump. Poets call it everything else.

## Anatomy First

The heart has four chambers: two atria and two ventricles. The right side receives deoxygenated blood from the body and sends it to the lungs. The left side receives oxygenated blood from the lungs and sends it to the rest of the body.

This dual circulation is one of evolution's most elegant solutions to the problem of keeping complex organisms alive.

Key structures include:

- **Sinoatrial (SA) node** — the heart's natural pacemaker
- **Atrioventricular (AV) node** — coordinates the delay between atrial and ventricular contractions
- **Bundle of His and Purkinje fibers** — the electrical highway of the heart
- **Coronary arteries** — the heart's own blood supply

What moves me most about cardiac anatomy is not any single structure. It is the **interdependence**. Every part relies on every other. The failure of one ripples through all.

## The Heart in Culture

Every language on earth has metaphors for the heart. In Nepali, we say *मन* (man) for the emotional heart and *मुटु* (mutu) for the organ. They are different words for the same idea: the center of a person.

Medicine teaches us that the heart is muscle and electricity. Philosophy teaches us that it is identity and feeling. The best doctors I have read about seem to hold both truths at once.

## What Cardiac Disease Looks Like in Nepal

Rheumatic heart disease (RHD) remains a significant public health challenge in South Asia. It begins with a streptococcal throat infection that, in vulnerable patients, triggers an immune response that attacks the heart valves.

Children who never received antibiotics for a sore throat can end up with damaged mitral valves years later.

This is a preventable disease. Penicillin costs almost nothing. Awareness costs even less. Yet thousands still suffer from it every year.

That gap between what is possible and what is happening — that is what I want to spend my career closing.
