# Why I Want to Be a Doctor

*Arjundhara, Jhapa — November 2024*

---

There is a small clinic near the edge of our town. It has peeling paint, a wooden bench outside, and a doctor who comes three days a week. I grew up watching people walk miles to sit on that bench.

Some of them never made it in time.

That image never left me. Not when I was studying late into the night, not when I was writing poems about longing and loss. Medicine, I came to understand, was not just a profession. It was a form of love.

## The Moment I Decided

It was not one single moment. It was a series of small ones.

My grandmother once said to me, *"There are hands that cut and hands that heal. Choose yours carefully."* I did not understand it then. I do now.

Watching local healthcare workers move with calm urgency, carry the weight of lives in their instruments, and speak softly even in crisis — I saw in them something I wanted to become.

## Medicine and Meaning

I have been reading about the history of medicine lately. From Hippocrates to Ibn Sina, from Florence Nightingale to present-day frontline workers in Nepal — the thread that runs through all of them is **service**.

Not fame. Not wealth. Service.

A doctor in a rural hill district of Nepal earns far less than a doctor in Kathmandu. But the lives they touch are just as real. The tears of relief they receive are just as warm.

That is where I want to be.

## What I Am Doing About It

Currently, I am preparing for MBBS entrance examinations. Biology is my closest companion, and Physics has become less of an enemy over time. The road is long and full of practice papers, but every question I get right feels like one step closer.

I also write. Poetry and stories ground me. They remind me that beyond the biochemistry and anatomy diagrams, medicine is ultimately about stories — the story of a patient, a family, a community trying to survive and thrive.
